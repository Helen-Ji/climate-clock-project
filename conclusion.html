Conclusion

Beyond the Countdown

The Climate Clock is powerful because it makes climate crisis visible — but visibility alone is not enough.

Main Argument

What My Project Ultimately Argues

My project argues that the Climate Clock in Union Square is an important and effective public media object because it transforms climate science into something visible, urgent, and difficult to ignore. In a city full of noise, speed, and distraction, the clock stands out by turning climate change into a massive public countdown. It gives abstract scientific knowledge a dramatic form and reminds people that climate change is not a distant issue, but something unfolding in the present.

At the same time, my project also argues that the Climate Clock has clear limitations. A countdown can create urgency, but it cannot fully explain what climate crisis means in everyday life. It does not show who is affected first, how vulnerability is distributed unequally, what kinds of emotions climate crisis produces, or what specific actions people should take after seeing it. Many people notice the clock without understanding it, and even people who do understand it may still feel emotionally distant from it. In this sense, the Climate Clock succeeds in making climate change visible, but not always fully legible, meaningful, or actionable.

My solution is not to reject data-based climate communication, but to expand it. Climate communication can become more effective when numbers are combined with stories, images, testimony, public interaction, and concrete examples of lived consequence. Instead of relying only on abstraction, public media should connect climate data to human experience: heat in apartments, smoke in the air, flooding in neighborhoods, and feelings of grief, anxiety, and responsibility. It should also give people clearer pathways for response, such as ways to learn more, talk with others, participate in collective action, or support environmental initiatives. In other words, the most effective communication does not replace data with emotion — it brings them together.

What data does well

Data matters

Data gives climate change measurable form. It helps define thresholds, track trends, communicate urgency, and connect public discourse to scientific knowledge. Without data, climate crisis can seem vague, invisible, or easy to deny. The Climate Clock depends on this strength: its number carries authority because it is linked to modeling, carbon budgets, and scientific interpretation.

What data cannot do alone

Emotion matters too

But climate crisis is not only a matter of measurement. It is also lived through fear, discomfort, uncertainty, grief, and responsibility. A person may understand a number intellectually without feeling its significance deeply. That is why emotional communication is also essential. Images, stories, sensory experience, and personal testimony can help people connect abstract knowledge to lived reality. Data can tell us that the crisis is real; emotion can help make that reality meaningful.

Climate issues need to be communicated from many angles at once. Data is important because it provides evidence, scale, and scientific credibility. Emotion is important because it helps people relate to that evidence, interpret it in human terms, and understand why it matters. When climate communication depends only on numbers, it risks becoming cold, distant, and difficult to absorb. When it combines data with feeling, it becomes more capable of creating understanding, empathy, and action.

Suggestions

How Climate Change Can Be Communicated More Effectively

Based on this project, I believe that public climate communication should become more layered and more human-centered. First, numerical information should be accompanied by plain language explanations so that people immediately understand what they are looking at. Second, public climate media should connect numbers to concrete consequences, not just in global terms but in local and everyday ones. Third, communication should create stronger emotional pathways through visual design, storytelling, or firsthand accounts, so that urgency is not only seen but felt. Finally, public projects should offer some sense of what comes next: not necessarily a single answer, but at least a clearer sense of agency, direction, and possible response.

The goal is not simply to alarm people. The goal is to help them understand climate crisis more fully: scientifically, socially, emotionally, and politically. A better climate message is one that informs, moves, and guides at the same time.

Call to Action

Protect the Environment Together

Climate change is not only a scientific issue for experts or a distant problem for the future. It is already shaping the world people live in now. That is why protecting the environment must become part of everyday awareness and collective responsibility. We need to pay attention not only to the numbers, but also to the lives, places, and futures those numbers represent.

Everyone can begin somewhere: by learning more, by talking about climate issues with friends and family, by supporting environmental action in their communities, by reducing waste, by demanding stronger policies, and by refusing to treat climate crisis as background noise. Public media like the Climate Clock can remind us that time matters. What happens next depends on whether we choose to respond.
