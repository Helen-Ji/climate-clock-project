Interactive Games

Play With the Problem

These games test what climate numbers make visible, what they leave out, and how public visibility does not always become public understanding.

Game 1

Guess the Number

Climate change is often communicated through numbers. But do numbers actually explain themselves?

1.5°C

What does this number mean?

Numbers can focus attention quickly. But attention is not the same as understanding.

Purpose of this game

This game shows that a climate number can summarize danger very efficiently, but it cannot fully explain who is harmed first, how that harm is felt in daily life, or what action should follow. A number like 1.5°C or 420 ppm sounds precise and authoritative, but it often hides the human, political, and emotional dimensions of crisis. The Climate Clock depends on this same tension: numerical urgency is powerful, but it does not tell the whole story.

Find the Climate Clock

Congratulations — you found the Climate Clock. But finding it is not the same as understanding it. Public visibility does not automatically create public meaning.

Purpose of this game

This section shows that public visibility is not the same as public understanding. Even if the Climate Clock is physically large, illuminated, and installed in a public square, people may still walk past it, overlook it, misunderstand what it measures, or not know what to do after seeing it. The problem is not just whether the clock is visible. The deeper issue is whether the message becomes legible, emotionally meaningful, and politically actionable in the middle of urban distraction and overload.
