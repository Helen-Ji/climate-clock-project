Relevance

How the Climate Clock Connects to Course Concepts

This page explains how the NYC Climate Clock relates to four major keywords: framing, hypocognition, prognostic politics, and the rhetorical functions of data.

My project is related to the following keywords: framing, hypocognition, the rhetorical functions of data, and prognostic politics. These concepts help explain why the Climate Clock is such a powerful but also limited form of public climate communication.

Keyword 1

Framing

George Lakoff’s research on framing theory has been especially helpful for this project. He argues that facts alone do not tell the whole story. People understand issues through frames, and facts that do not fit existing frames are often overlooked. In other words, people need a ready-made framework in order to make sense of a complex issue like global warming.

I think the Climate Clock tries to provide exactly this kind of framework. Instead of presenting charts or technical reports, it transforms climate change into a massive public countdown. By giving the crisis a memorable and emotionally resonant structure, the clock makes climate change easier to grasp in public space.

At the same time, Lakoff’s work also helps explain the limitations of the Climate Clock. If people understand climate change only as a countdown, other ways of understanding it may be pushed aside. The issue becomes framed primarily as “time running out,” while broader questions about justice, lived experience, and collective responsibility may receive less attention.

Keyword 2

Hypocognition

I also think the Climate Clock reflects the concept of hypocognition. When I walked through Union Square and asked my friends or random passersby about it, most people did not understand what the clock meant at first. This made me realize that for many people, climate change remains vague and distant, something they know exists but do not fully connect to their own daily lives.

In this sense, the Climate Clock fills one important gap in public understanding by making urgency visible. It provides a stronger frame for climate crisis than many people already have. However, it seems to do little more than highlight urgency. It does not, by itself, explain inequality, responsibility, adaptation, or the different ways climate crisis is lived across different communities.

So while the clock may reduce one kind of cognitive gap, many others remain. It helps people see that climate change matters now, but it does not fully provide the concepts needed to understand why it matters differently for different groups of people.

Keyword 3

Prognostic Politics

I believe the Climate Clock is also closely related to prognostic politics because it is specifically designed with the future in mind. Mike Hulme argues that climate change is a classic example of “post-normal science,” where uncertainty, high stakes, and urgent decision-making all exist at the same time. This idea aligns closely with how the Climate Clock works.

The clock does not just tell us what climate science knows. It translates that knowledge into a public timeline for action. It asks people to think politically in relation to a future threshold: what will happen if action is delayed, how much time remains, and what kinds of decisions should be made now.

This is prognostic politics because it organizes public feeling around the future. It turns scientific knowledge into a countdown that structures anticipation, urgency, and anxiety. In this way, the Climate Clock makes the future feel immediate, even if it does not tell us exactly what action should follow.

Keyword 4

The Rhetorical Functions of Data

Finally, the Climate Clock is closely linked to the rhetorical functions of data. Paul Edwards notes that climate knowledge is always conveyed through models and data systems, because the global climate cannot be grasped directly through everyday experience. He argues that critical guidance and decisions about climate change are fundamentally dependent on modeling.

This means that while the numbers on the Climate Clock appear simple and direct, they actually condense a long process of modeling, carbon budget calculation, and scientific interpretation. Edwards also points out that models are not used only to predict the future; they are also essential for generating the usable global data on which climate knowledge depends.

Because of this, the Climate Clock is more than an informational device. It transforms complex climate science into a visible and emotionally impactful public symbol. Through this project, I want to explore whether this kind of data rhetoric can truly foster understanding and encourage people to act, or whether it risks reducing climate crisis to a number that feels urgent but still abstract.

Conclusion

Why These Concepts Matter for My Project

Together, these four concepts help explain both the power and the limits of the Climate Clock. It frames climate change in a clear and dramatic way, but it may narrow how people understand the crisis. It fills one gap in public awareness, but other forms of misunderstanding remain. It turns climate science into a future- oriented political warning, yet it does not fully show what action should look like. And while it uses data in a rhetorically powerful way, it also raises the question of whether data alone can create deep understanding.

This is why the Climate Clock is such an important case study for my project: it makes climate change visible, but it also reveals the limits of visibility, quantification, and urgency when they are not connected to fuller human, social, and political meaning.
